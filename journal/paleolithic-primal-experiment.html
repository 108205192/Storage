Own Responses Received & Others Researched

Not literal reconstruction.
I do not swear by this, even though I may already sound like or qualify as an elitist or 'paleotard'. (I have not tried to convince anyone to try it, but I have had difficulty hiding my general excitement over just how awesome this diet is, as well as letting go of even a lingering desire to see those I am living with ditch what they are downing...) The experiment has only recently begun, yet the research into it has extended back half a year, and already I have:
  1. Faced questions over no grains.
  2. Received scrutiny as to what I am doing and why.
  3. Had difficulty dining out due to lack of unprocessed, whole food choices.
    • (Except buffets and salad bars. They can be awesome, depending.)
  4. Received comments about the 'stupidity' of this, or why I will not eat [this] or [that].
  5. Encountered "but cavemen didn't have [this] or do [that]" and so on, and each time I have stated in response: "Metabolic reconstruction, not literal, although physical fitness and natural living is a big part of the vibration inherent in paleo / primal.
I have found that the simplest thing is to say that I don't touch gluten anymore and "eat clean" / no processed food, and then proceed to pick and choose what is or is not paleo / primal (such as selecting meat but not using any bread), because trying to explain 'paleo' would probably be too complicated and/or long for conversations, and in fact, in one individual's experience, it has been nothing but frustrating:
My issue here is that they ALWAYS compliment me. They ALWAYS ask how I've achieved such great results, they ALWAYS want to know about my diet.

Then, the moment I tell them, they suddenly turn from all "You look great and are really healthy." to "That shit is unhealthy, stop it right now." in like a two-second flip. Ugh!
(Not everyone, though—as knowledge of this spreads, even some doctors become supportive of it, such as those listed at Low-Carb Doctors.)

Anyway, I can say that, for the first time in my life, I actually enjoy what I am making and eating, but more importantly, my body does, with no complaints of any kind, yet positive changes include vastly improved endurance, mental acuity, and stamina. Additionally, I also had a ton of fun spending a considerable amount of time researching this subject, arguably more than 40 hours over the course of two weeks.

I have not been at this very long, only nearly one month, yet I already see and feel why this is considered a lifestyle—a way of life—and not a diet.

Overall? Fascinating, really.

Postscript: This article was originally published September 2012 after over a month of research. Back then and prior I averaged 120 pounds / 54.43 kilograms. Today, July 2013: I average 135 / 61.23, marking the first significant weight increase in my life since puberty.
